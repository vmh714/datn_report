## Classification of Fallers and Non-Fallers in Older Adults Using Electrical IMU Signal for Gait Analysis and Explainable Deep Learning 

**Ahmed Alqurashi[a] , Abdullah Alharthi[b,] Mohammed M. Alammar[c] , Nasser Aldosari[d] , Abdulrahman Al Ayidh[ e]** 

aDepartment of Electrical Engineering, Umm Al Qura University, Makkah, Saudi Arabia, akqurashi@uqu.edu.sa bDepartment of Electrical Engineering, King Khalid University, Abha, Saudi Arabia, asalharthi@kku.edu.sa cDepartment of Electrical Engineering, King Khalid University, Abha, Saudi Arabia, mmalamar@kku.edu.sa d Clinical Simulation Administration, King Abdullah Medical City, Makkah, Saudi Arabia , aldosari.n@kamc.med.sa eDepartment of Electrical Engineering, King Khalid University, Abha, Saudi Arabia; aalaid@kku.edu.sa 

## **Abstract** 

Falls among older adults constitute a major public health concern, and effective prevention requires identifying biomechanical and functional factors that elevate risk. This study employed inertial measurement units (IMUs) to collect 6-channel acceleration and angular velocity recordings from 163 individuals aged 70-98 years, categorized by fall history and stratified into three age groups. Participants completed 30-minute walking tasks, and the resulting stride-level gait signals were used to retrospectively classify fall history (fallers versus non-fallers) and age-related gait patterns. Traditional machine-learning algorithms were trained on aggregated temporal statistics (mean, standard deviation, PCA-reduced representations), while deep-learning models (Long Short-Term Memory networks and Convolutional Neural Networks) processed raw stride timeseries directly. Models were evaluated under data-level 10-fold cross-validation (Experiment 1) and subject-wise crossvalidation with held-out participants (Experiment 2). Under Experiment 1, LSTM achieved 95% accuracy for fall-history classification and CNN attained 96%, substantially outperforming traditional approaches. Under the more stringent Experiment 2, CNN maintained 92% fall-classification accuracy, confirming robust generalization. To demonstrate model capability on multi-class problems, both architectures were also evaluated on age-group classification (70-79, 80-89, 90-99 years) and subject identification, achieving strong performance across tasks. Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME) was applied to the CNN model for fall history classification, the primary focus of this work, to reveal that classification decisions are driven primarily by stance phase irregularities (83.8% contribution in fallers versus 61.7% in non-fallers), particularly during terminal stance and mid-stance, which were post-hoc linked to known fall-risk factors such as impaired weight transfer, balance instability, and reduced foot clearance. These findings demonstrate that IMU-based deep learning can accurately classify fall history and provide candidate gait-phase patterns associated with fall-history classification that may inform prospective fall-risk prediction models pending independent and longitudinal validation. 

**Keywords-** Fall history classification; Gait-based biomarkers; Inertial measurement units (IMUs); Machine learning; deep learning; explainable artificial intelligence (XAI); Fall-risk stratification; Gait phase analysis; Older adults. 

## **1. Introduction** 

Falls among older adults (≥65 years) are a major public health concern due to their high prevalence and severe consequences [1]. According to the World Health Organization, unintentional injury-related mortality due to falls is the second leading cause of such fatalities worldwide [2]. The population of adults aged 65 years and older comprised 10% of the world's population in 2022, i.e., 800 million individuals, which is likely to increase to 16% of the world's population by 2050, i.e., an absolute figure of 1.5 billion individuals [3]. Hence, it is of utmost importance to address the risk of falling to reduce the everincreasing burden of health care costs as well as the rising costs of living. The risk of falling is the result of the complex interplay of physiological, environmental, and behavioral factors [4]. Frailty, functional limitations, and abnormal gait patterns such as reduced stride length, stride time variability, and impaired balance are strong predictors of fall risk. Hence, biomechanical analysis of gait patterns has been found to be a promising tool to detect individuals at a higher risk of falling in the future [5]. 

The major difference in the methodology of the studies lies in the classification of individuals as fallers or non-fallers on the basis of the history of falling and the prediction of the risk of falling in the future. The classification of individuals as fallers or non-fallers on the basis of the history of falling helps to delineate the biomechanical as well as the spatiotemporal parameters of the gait that differentiate the individuals in the two groups. The identification of such discriminative biomarkers is the most

important requirement to develop a prospective fall risk prediction model. Recent studies have revealed that the proper classification of individuals into different groups using gait analysis techniques such as computer vision, motion sensors, and walkway-based systems can accurately predict the risk of falling by identifying the gait parameters that are strongly associated with the history of falling [6-8]. The history of falling has been recognized to be one of the strongest predictors for future falls in older adults, making it essential to identify gait-based signatures associated with past falls to predict future fall risks effectively [9-11]. Therefore, it is essential to develop classification-based biomarkers for predicting fall risks in older adults through this study [10, 12, 13]. The classification-based biomarkers identified in this study can be used to develop effective prediction models to predict fall risks in older adults in the future. The main objective of this study was to develop effective classification-based biomarkers to predict fall risks in older adults by focusing solely on the classification problem. 

Advances in wearable technology, especially inertial measurement unit (IMU), have enabled accurate monitoring of walking patterns in older adults in real-world settings [14]. The data obtained using IMUs have provided detailed information regarding foot strikes, stride time, walking velocity, and gait variability in older adults, providing useful insights into walking patterns in older adults [15, 16]. However, it has been challenging to translate the information obtained using IMUs into effective fall-risk prediction in older adults. 

Machine learning techniques have been increasingly used to develop effective models to predict walking patterns and detect abnormal walking patterns in older adults with a high risk of falling. Classical machine learning techniques, including Random Forest (RF), K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), and tree-based techniques, have been widely used to develop effective models to classify walking patterns in older adults with fall risks. Deep learning techniques, including LSTM networks and CNNs, have been used to develop effective walking pattern classification models with high accuracy in older adults with fall risks. However, most studies have been limited by sample size and have failed to perform systematic comparisons between machine learning techniques and have not evaluated the contribution of features to walking patterns in older adults with fall risks. Age stratification plays a crucial role in assessing the risk of falls, and this is because the pattern of gait degradation and fall risk varies considerably across age decades in the elderly population. The age group of 70-79 years shows early signs of gait degradation, including slight decreases in gait speed and stride length [6, 17]. The 80-89 age group, on the other hand, shows higher stride time variability and postural instability [9, 18]. The oldest age group, 90-99, shows severe biomechanical degradation, including greatly decreased foot clearance and irregular stepping patterns, resulting in exponentially higher fall risks [9]. The classification of the age groups using machine learning algorithms capable of distinguishing between the gait signatures of different age groups enables personalized risk assessment, and this helps clinicians to adopt fall prevention strategies suitable for the biomechanical characteristics of a particular age group [17, 19]. Age group classification also validates the effectiveness of the gait metrics in capturing physiologically significant degradation in the aging continuum, thus validating their clinical effectiveness in assessing the risk of falls in the elderly population [6, 18]. The present study attempts to fill this gap by utilizing a public domain database of 163 participants, ranging from 70 to 99 years of age, and categorized them into fallers and non-fallers, and further divided them into three age groups: 70-79, 80-89, and 90-99. The participants wore foot-mounted IMUs and performed walking trials, and the continuous 6-channel acceleration and angular velocity data from the foot-mounted IMUs was recorded. The performance of various machine learning algorithms, including Random Forest, K-Nearest Neighbors, and LightGBM, and two deep learning architectures, LSTM and CNN, was evaluated for their effectiveness in classifying the participants into fallers and non-fallers and into the three age groups, and their performance was evaluated in terms of accuracy, precision, and F1 score. The effectiveness of the IMU data in capturing the temporal and spatial characteristics of the gait was evaluated using Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations (LIME), and this study has contributed to the field in the following four areas:  **Superiority of deep learning models** : The performance of temporal (LSTM) and spatial (CNN) models was found to be superior to traditional models for IMU-based fall history classification, obtaining 95-96% accuracy in data-level cross-validation and 87-92% in more rigorous subject-level validation. 

- **Multi-task performance** : The performance of both models was found to be robust for multi-class classification, validating their potential for generalization in various gait-based prediction tasks. 

- **Data representation** : The effect of using temporal and aggregated feature representations was systematically evaluated, and the performance of deep learning models on raw data was found to be substantially higher than traditional models on aggregated features. 

- **Feature interpretability:** The primary contribution of this study was the feature interpretability of LIME on the CNN model for fall history classification, which was found to reveal important biomechanical gait phase characteristics, including the dominance of the stance phase in fallers (83.8%) compared to non-fallers (61.7%).

There is considerable evidence in the literature regarding the multifactorial nature of fall risk in older adults. To facilitate the presentation of the relevant literature in a logical structure, the following review is organized into five thematic domains: physical/biomechanical factors, psychological/cognitive factors, functional assessment tools, sensor-based technologies, and

## **2.1.  Physical and Biomechanical Factors** 

Frailty and biomechanical factors are the primary predictors of fall risk in older adults. Fried's frailty phenotype, consisting of unintentional weight loss, fatigue, low physical activity, slowness, and weakness, has been consistently linked to the risk of falling in older adults [21-29]. Gait analysis has been shown to offer insight into the mechanisms of motor control, balance, and biomechanical factors in the elderly. Abnormalities in stride length, stride time variability, walking speed, and foot placement have been linked to fall risk in older adults, as well as mediolateral sway and trunk rotation [24]. The natural aging process causes flexibility in the joints, muscle strength, and neuromuscular coordination to decline, which in turn causes abnormal gait patterns in older adults. Interventions such as resistance training, balance training, and tai chi have been found to be effective in improving physical decline and fall risk in older adults; however, it is vital to detect the abnormal gait patterns in older adults to effectively intervene in the problem [27-29]. 

## **2.2. Psychological and Cognitive Influences** 

Additionally, the fear of falling and cognitive impairment tend to interact synergistically, which affects the fall risks of elderly patients. The Short Falls Efficacy Scale-International (FES-I) is commonly used for measuring the level of fear of falling, which, if high, is associated with reduced mobility and muscular deconditioning [30]. Cognitive impairment, which can be assessed by the Global Deterioration Scale (GDS), affects attention, executive ability, and spatial ability, thereby increasing the risks of falls [31]. Longitudinal studies suggest that multiple falls can cause cognitive deterioration, while cognitive interventions can reduce the incidence of falls. Most of the studies on psychological, cognitive, biomechanical, and other factors tend to be conducted independently, which highlights the importance of assessing both mental and physical factors. **2.3. Functional Assessments for Fall Risk** Conventional clinical tests play an important role in assessing the fall risks of elderly patients. The Timed Up and Go (TUG) test, Short Physical Performance Battery (SPPB), and gait speed tests are commonly used for assessing the fall risks of elderly patients, as they are easy, cheap, and reliable [32]. These tests are commonly used for assessing the fall risks of elderly patients, especially in primary care settings, as they require little equipment, can be performed within a short period, and provide reliable results. However, the results obtained by performing these tests provide only limited information about the mobility of elderly patients, as they may not be able to capture temporary changes in gait patterns, which may be contextdependent. Besides, the subjective nature of the test results obtained by performing such tests makes them less reliable for continuous monitoring of elderly patients. The possibility of enhancing the results obtained by performing such tests by including sensor-based technology is being explored, where a sensor-based model has been developed for predicting the results of the TUG test for improving the assessment of functional mobility of elderly patients, although there is no consensus on how this can be achieved [33]. **2.4. Sensor‑Based Technologies** The development of wearable sensors has revolutionized the field of gait analysis. The inertial measurement unit (IMU), consisting of accelerometers and gyroscopes, allows for the constant, precise measurement of spatiotemporal gait parameters in natural environments [34-36]. The G-STRIDE system, for example, uses a smartphone-based IMU to accurately calculate stride length and walking velocity in natural environments [37, 38]. Environmental sensors such as pressure mats, motion sensors, and video systems can also be employed to detect environmental hazards such as insufficient lighting or obstacles in the walking path [39-41]. However, the usage of gait analysis sensors is limited by factors such as the absence of standardized sensor placement, user compliance, privacy, the cost of using multiple sensors, and the lack of integration of the sensors into the workflow of clinicians. In addition, the sensors are mostly designed to work in isolation without the integration of physical, cognitive, or environmental factors. 

## **2.5. Machine‑Learning and AI Approaches** 

In the context of AI, there is growing interest in the application of artificial intelligence in understanding complex relationships in gait and fall risk data. Reviews indicate that machine-learning algorithms, such as Random Forest, XGBoost, and gradient boosting machines, attain accuracy in fall classification that is higher than 80–85% in classification tasks on multidimensional health data from older adults [9, 42]. Deep-learning algorithms, such as long short-term memory (LSTM) and CNNs, are shown to surpass traditional methods in gait and fall analysis by incorporating temporal and spatial patterns in gait data [6, 19]. The LSTM algorithm has reportedly achieved 92-96% accuracy for predicting fall risks based on temporal gait features [6, 43]. Another deep learning algorithm, CNN-LSTM, has reportedly achieved 95-98% accuracy for fall detection and frailty classification [19, 44]. However, there is always the concern that such algorithms remain "black boxes" for

Some of the challenges associated with AI algorithms include imbalance [9, 42, 63 ], which may be addressed by emerging algorithms such as federated learning, transfer learning, and sensor fusion [19]. 

Overall, the literature on fall risks has highlighted the multifactorial nature of fall risks, as well as the advances made in wearables technology and AI algorithms for analysis of data sets. However, there is still a dearth of unified systems for integrating physical, cognitive, and environmental data sets for real-time, personalized fall risks. This study aims to bridge the gap between theory and practice by integrating IMU-based gait metrics with psychological and cognitive data sets, as well as deep learning algorithms for fall risks assessment.

## **3.2. Data Preprocessing and Feature Extraction** 

The raw IMU data was then checked for missing values and replaced using a nearest neighbor interpolation method. The continuous IMU data was segmented into individual stride windows using gait phase detection algorithms included in the GSTRIDE database. Each individual stride was then represented as a multivariate time series with dimensions [timesteps × 6 channels]. Here, six channels include triaxial linear acceleration (ax, ay, az) and triaxial angular velocity (ωx, ωy, ωz).  The timesteps vary according to stride duration (typically 80–120 samples, depending on walking speed). 

Three different IMU-based feature representations for traditional machine learning (FLAT, MEAN, STD) and a fourth representation for deep learning in its raw 2D form (time x channels) were created to evaluate the performance of different input conditions:

- **FLAT dataset** : For each individual stride, the 6-channel time series representation of dimension [timesteps x 6 channels] was transformed into a single 1D feature vector representation using a concatenation of all time points across all channels. For instance, for a particular stride with 100 time steps, the resulting feature vector length is 600 (100 x 6 channels). The flattened vectors were then standardized by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation computed across all training samples on a per-feature basis, where each time channel position is treated as a feature. Next, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was applied for dimensionality reduction from 480-720 features (i.e., 80-120 timesteps across strides) down to 50 principal components. The choice of 50 components is based on prior analysis, where it was determined that >95% of the cumulative explained variance could be retained while maintaining computational efficiency for grid search hyperparameter optimization of all traditional ML models. For the deep learning models (CNN, LSTM), the original unflattened stride time series [timesteps x 6] is used without any flattening or PCA. 

- **MEAN dataset** : The temporal mean of each of the six IMU channel signals is computed independently across the time axis for each stride, generating a feature vector of length 6: [mean(ax), mean(ay), mean(az), mean(ωx), mean(ωy), mean(ωz)]. This compact feature vector ignores the temporal dynamics of the data and is used exclusively for traditional ML models. 

- **STD dataset** : For each stride, we computed the temporal standard deviation of each of the six IMU channels independently (across the time axis). This produces a 6-element feature vector per stride: [std(ax), std(ay), std(az), std(ωx), std(ωy), std(ωz)], capturing the variability of IMU signals within each stride. This representation also discards temporal sequencing and was used exclusively for traditional machine learning models. 

 **2D dataset** Real-time fixed-size windows were used to extract fixed-size data from continuous IMU data, which were used as an input for CNN and LSTM models. This data set consists of 500 timesteps with a sampling rate of 100 Hz, which is equivalent to 5 seconds of continuous gait data. This data set has 6 channels of IMU data, including acceleration and gyroscope data. The reason for using a 5-second data window is that it can cover multiple gait cycles because a normal person takes 1.0 to 1.3 seconds to complete a single step. This method can cover different step lengths by using a fixedsize data window instead of a fixed-size step. There is no need to perform any domain-specific feature engineering because CNN and LSTM models can take 500x6 data directly after standard preprocessing. Figure 2. ## **3.3. Proposed Classification Framework** 

Figure 3 presents the end‑to‑end pipeline for faller/non‑faller and age‑group classification. IMU signals were preprocessed and segmented into individual gait cycles; these segments served as inputs to the classification models. We implemented two paradigms: (1) traditional machine-learning algorithms trained directly on three different representations of raw 6-channel IMU stride data, flattened vectors (FLAT), temporal mean statistics (MEAN), and temporal variability statistics (STD), as detailed in Section 3.2, without domain-specific gait feature engineering, and (2) deep-learning models that ingest raw stride time-series sequences [time_steps × 6 channels] and learn discriminative representations automatically. Specifically, traditional ML models were trained on: (a) PCA-reduced flattened stride vectors (FLAT, reduced to 50 principal components), (b) 6-element temporal mean vectors (MEAN), or (c) 6-element temporal variability vectors (STD). No domain-specific gait feature engineering procedures (e.g., computing stride length, cadence, or foot clearance from IMU signals) were applied; all models operated directly on aggregations or transformations of the raw 6-channel IMU readings, Finally the deep learning models are trained on the full 2D raw data. For interpretability, the framework incorporated LIME to identify which timeseries segments contributed most to each classification [61]. The LIME explanation involves approximating the complex model locally with a sparse linear surrogate by minimizing a locality-weighted loss plus a complexity penalty. The complexity penalty, inspired by lasso regularization, ensures that the surrogate remains simple by selecting only the most important features [61]. LIME identified which time-series segments of the raw 6-channel IMU data contributed most to each classification decision.

## **3.4. Machine-Learning Models** 

A series of supervised machine learning algorithms were used to identify and distinguish between fallers and non-fallers. Traditional machine learning algorithms, statistical models, and deep learning models such as CNN and LSTM were used in this study because of their proven ability to effectively analyze complex data such as gait patterns among older adults, as presented in Figure 2. The models used and their theories are presented below: 

## _**3.4.1. Traditional models:**_ 

Several machine-learning algorithms were employed, each offering distinct advantages. The Decision Tree (DT) is a non-parametric supervised method for classification and regression that predicts target values by learning simple decision rules from data features [51]. RF extends DTs by using an ensemble of trees, with the final prediction based on the mode of the classes predicted by individual trees, thereby enhancing robustness and accuracy [52]. KNN is a non-parametric method that classifies data points according to the majority class among their k nearest neighbors in feature space [53]. Naïve Bayes (NB) is a probabilistic classifier grounded in Bayes’ theorem, assuming strong independence between features for efficiency [54]. Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) is an iterative optimization method that updates model parameters incrementally based on gradients from a single or small batch of training examples [55]. eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) is a distributed gradient-boosting library that builds models sequentially to correct prior errors [56]. LightGBM is another tree-based gradientboosting framework optimized for scalability and performance on large, high-dimensional datasets [57]. CatBoost is a gradient-boosting algorithm designed for categorical features, using ordered boosting and target statistics to mitigate overfitting and improve predictive accuracy [58]. _**3.4.2. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN):**_ Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are a class of deep-learning models well suited for processing grid-like data such as images or time series [59]. CNNs have been widely applied to fall detection and gait analysis due to their ability to automatically learn hierarchical feature representations from raw input data without extensive manual feature extraction [48, 49, 51]. In gait analysis, CNNs can automatically learn hierarchical feature representations from raw IMU data, capturing subtle spatial patterns and variations in gait metrics that may indicate fall risk [48]. By leveraging convolutional layers, CNNs detect local dependencies and invariant features within gait data, improving classification between fallers and non-fallers [48, 51]. The 1D CNN model is composed of three Conv1D layers with batch normalization. To be specific, the three Conv1D layers are: a Conv1D layer with 64 filters of size 5x1 and same padding followed by a MaxPooling1D layer with a pool size of 2; a Conv1D layer with 128 filters of size 5x1 and same padding; a Conv1D layer with 128 filters of size 3x1 followed by a MaxPooling1D layer with a pool size of 2. Finally, the feature maps produced by these layers are combined using a GlobalAveragePooling1D layer followed by a Dropout layer with a dropout rate of 0.5, a Dense layer with 128 units and ReLU activation function, a Dropout layer with a dropout rate of 0.2, and a softmax output layer. Such a hierarchical structure allows the earlier layers to learn local kinematic features (e.g., stride-to-stride variations and foot strikes) and then combine these local features to learn global gait signatures. The model was trained using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.002, β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999 for 200 epochs with a batch size of 100 and a validation set of 10%.

The LSTM network architecture allows for the exploration of gait data and the identification of temporal patterns and trends, which are critical for detecting anomalies related to fall risk.

## **3.5. Model Training and Evaluation** 

The models were trained and evaluated under two complementary experimental protocols, designed to assess both general performance and subject-level generalization. 

**Experiment 1 (Data-level 10-fold cross-validation):** In the first experiment, the dataset was evaluated using standard 10fold cross-validation based on data splits. The full dataset was randomly partitioned into ten folds, with nine folds used for training and one fold reserved for testing in each iteration. This protocol assesses performance under conventional samplelevel partitioning and provides a baseline for comparison. Model hyperparameters were optimized using grid search, and the complete set of tuned parameters and search ranges for all models is reported in Supplementary Table S2 to ensure full reproducibility. 

**Experiment 2 (Subject-wise 10-fold cross-validation withheld-out subjects):** In the second experiment, a stricter subject-level evaluation was employed to assess generalization to unseen individuals. Subjects were assigned to 10 mutually exclusive folds using stratified GroupKFold splitting (stratified by fall history and age group), ensuring that all 163 subjects appeared in exactly one test fold. Each fold's training set comprised approximately 147 subjects, and the test set comprised approximately 16 subjects. No subject appeared in both training and test sets within any fold. This protocol eliminates subject leakage and provides a more realistic assessment of model performance in real-world deployment scenarios. 

Across both experiments, models were trained independently on the FLAT, MEAN, and STD representations, while deeplearning models (CNNs and LSTMs) were trained directly on the raw 2D data. This design enabled a systematic evaluation of the impact of data representation and transformation on classification performance. ## **3.6. Explainable AI (XAI) Analysis Using LIME** 

Deep-learning models often behave as black boxes, limiting clinical adoption. To enhance interpretability, we employed LIME exclusively to the CNN model for fall history classification [61, 62]. While age-group classification and subject identification tasks were included to demonstrate the models' capability to handle multi-class problems beyond binary fall history classification, we focused explainability analysis on fall history classification as this task has the most direct clinical relevance for fall-risk assessment and prevention. Age-group and subject identification analyses validated model generalization but were not the primary clinical objective. LIME is a post-hoc method that approximates the behavior of complex classifiers by fitting a simple, interpretable surrogate model in the local vicinity of a given input [61]. Importantly, LIME is model agnostic; it treats the classifier as a black box and relies on input perturbations to observe corresponding output changes [61]. For a given input and a target gait class, the model's prediction is locally approximated by a linear surrogate model expressed as: g(x') = w₀ + ∑i=1[n] wᵢ x'ᵢ (9) where x' denotes a perturbed version of the original input x, and the coefficients wᵢ represent the contribution of each feature to the prediction. To compute these coefficients, LIME minimizes an objective function that balances fidelity to the original model and the simplicity of the surrogate model: ξ(x) = argming∈G L(f, g, πₓ) + Ω(g) (10) In this equation, L(f, g, πₓ) quantifies the loss (or discrepancy) between the predictions of the complex model f and the surrogate model g within the locality defined by x, where π‑ is a proximity measure that weights perturbed samples according to their similarity to x. The term Ω(g) imposes a complexity penalty, thereby ensuring that the surrogate model remains sufficiently simple for interpretation [61, 62]. We implemented LIME using _LimeTabularExplainer_ on a flattened representation of the CNN input. Each 500-timestep × 6-channel IMU window was reshaped into a 3000-dimensional feature vector (time×channel features), and LIME generated explanations at this time×channel granularity. We used _num_samples=5000_ perturbed samples per explanation, reported the top num_features=30 contributing time×channel features for the target class, disabled continuous variable discretization ( _discretize_continuous=False_ ), and fixed the random seed ( _random_state=2024_ ) for reproducibility. Features were internally labeled as _T{time_ms}ms {channel}_ (e.g., "T220ms Acc X") to enable direct mapping back to the original time axis and IMU channel. Because kernel parameters were not explicitly overridden in our implementation, default LIME proximity kernel settings were used. The representative LIME explanations shown in Results were generated for two high-confidence, correctly classified test windows (one faller, one non-faller) as illustrative examples of the model's decision-making patterns. 

In this study, LIME is employed exclusively on the CNN model for the classification task of fall history (fallers vs. nonfallers). This is because the hierarchical nature of the CNN model, where successive layers are employed for local kinematic patterns and global gait signatures, allows for a better mapping between influential raw IMU signal segments and clinically recognized gait phases. 

Two explanation modalities were created, each providing a different level of understanding on how the CNN model classified the test data into fallers and non-fallers: 

## **(1) Gait Phase-Level Importance Scores:** 

To improve the interpretability of the model, feature attributions from the LIME model are aggregated based on the temporal boundaries defined by seven gait phases: Heel Strike (A: 0–2%), Loading Response (B: 2–12%), Mid-Stance (C: 12– 31%), Terminal Stance (D: 31–50%), Pre-Swing (E: 50–60%), Initial & Mid-Swing (F: 60–87%), and Terminal Swing (G: 87–100%). These phases correspond to standard biomechanical subdivisions of the gait cycle; phase boundaries were mapped to the input window using normalized gait-cycle percentage definitions to support phase-level aggregation The gait phase boundaries are extracted from the GSTRIDE database annotations for each test case and are used to determine the feature attributions from the LIME model that fall under each gait phase. The importance score for each gait phase is calculated by

aggregating the absolute value of feature attributions that fall under each gait phase and normalizing it by the total feature attribution value. This allows for the quantification and determination of the contribution of each clinically recognized gait phase to the CNN model classification outcome. 

## **(2) Feature-Level Importance Weights:** 

For finer detail, LIME also provided feature-level attributions over the stride sequence input to the CNN, emphasizing time-channel pairs that were most important to the classification decision. The feature level corresponds to specific time points (t) and IMU channel (c), allowing us to identify key time points in the signal that were important to the CNN’s decision (e.g., “T2.22s-Acc X” indicates acceleration in the X-axis at 2.22 seconds). 

## **Post-Hoc Biomechanical Interpretation:** 

The post-hoc interpretation of LIME attributions utilized the available biomechanical gait parameters provided in the GSTRIDE database to qualitatively associate key segments in the IMU signal with key characteristics in the gait signal (e.g., stride time variability, foot clearance, and weight transfer characteristics). However, LIME was used to interpret the CNN’s decisions over the raw 6-channel IMU signal without access to any pre-computed gait parameters. This ensures that LIME explanations are faithful to what the CNN learned over raw sensor data and verifies that the CNN was able to learn clinically relevant patterns without any circular reasoning.

## **4.5. Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) Results Using LIME** 

Deep-learning models can exhibit limited transparency, which may hinder clinical interpretation even when predictive performance is strong. To improve interpretability and assess whether the CNN’s fall-history decisions align with biomechanically plausible gait structure, we applied Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME) to the CNN model for fall-history classification [61, 62]. LIME provides local explanations by approximating the model’s behavior around a single prediction using a sparse linear surrogate model, returning signed feature contributions that support (positive; green) or oppose (negative; red) the displayed class prediction [61, 62].

To present these explanations in a clinically interpretable form, we report LIME results using two complementary visualization strategies. First, phase-annotated time-series visualizations (Figure 15a,c) contextualize IMU dynamics within the gait cycle. Second, gait-phase importance charts (Figure 15b,d) summarize the aggregated contribution of each gait phase to the CNN’s decision for the displayed instance. The pronounced terminal stance contribution is biomechanically significant. Terminal stance represents the critical transition period where body weight shifts over the supporting leg, the ankle plantarflexes, and the foot prepares for toe-off to generate forward propulsion [32, 35]. Proper joint mechanics during this phase, ankle plantarflexion, knee extension, hip extension, and coordinated activation of plantar flexors, are essential for efficient energy transfer and balance maintenance. Dysfunction or irregularities during terminal stance may be associated with inefficient propulsion, increased energy expenditure, compromised balance stability, and altered weight transfer patterns, all of which are established biomechanical precursors to falls [32, 50]. 

The disproportionately low swing phase contribution in fallers (16.2%), compared to 38.3% in non-fallers, suggests that the CNN model prioritizes stance phase instabilities when identifying fall risk. This pattern is consistent with findings that stance phase time, double support time, and stance phase variability are key discriminators between fallers and non-fallers [46, 50, 58]. However, the non-zero swing phase contribution indicates that swing phase irregularities, particularly reduced foot clearance and asymmetric limb advancement, still play a secondary role in fall classification [33, 39, 66].

By identifying which gait phases dominate each individual's fall-risk profile, LIME-enhanced CNN predictions enable personalized, biomechanically informed rehabilitation prescriptions that target the specific movement deficits underlying elevated fall risk. 

## **4.9. Methodological Considerations** 

The variability in LIME contribution magnitudes across individual predictions reflects instance-specific decision boundaries and the local complexity of the feature space around each prediction. LIME weights are inherently local approximations; wider ranges indicate that the model relied on stronger feature contrasts to achieve classification certainty for that particular instance. This variability is methodologically expected and does not compromise the validity of the explanations. 

The examples presented in Figures 15 and 16 represent typical LIME explanation patterns observed across the test set. These representative cases illustrate the consistent pattern whereby fallers exhibit high stance phase dominance (particularly terminal stance) while non-fallers show more balanced stance-swing contributions. This observation aligns with established fall-risk literature and provides a foundation for future systematic quantification of LIME attribution patterns across larger cohorts and prospective follow-up studies. 

Importantly, LIME operated directly on the raw 6-channel IMU time series without access to pre-computed gait parameters (e.g., stride time, foot clearance, cadence) available in the GSTRIDE database. The biomechanical interpretations presented here constitute post-hoc qualitative linkage between LIME-identified influential IMU signal segments and known gait characteristics, informed by established biomechanics literature and the kinematic descriptions available in the GSTRIDE database documentation. This approach ensures that LIME explanations reflect the model's learned representations from raw sensor data rather than engineered features, thereby validating that the CNN autonomously discovered clinically relevant patterns.

Implications for Clinical Applications and Fall Prevention Strategies:** The present findings indicate that machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) models, especially Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and 1D Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), can support screening and triage for fall risk by extracting clinically plausible gait patterns from wearable IMU data. In our results, operating points favored high sensitivity with strong specificity (Figures 6-10), and LIME analyses (Figure 13a-d) showed which segments of the raw IMU signals drove classification decisions (CNN → local spatial patterns; LSTM → temporal patterns). Wearable IMU systems paired with DL inference can enable continuous or near-real-time screening in the community, long-term care, or outpatient settings, surfacing early deviations in gait that precede clinical events [12, 13]. In practice, sites should deploy calibrated risk scores (rather than binary labels) and adopt explicit action thresholds (e.g., screen/watch/reassure) chosen with decision-curve analysis and local prevalence in mind. Reporting PPV/NPV at site-specific prevalences and maintaining simple, interpretable dashboards can keep teams aligned on progress and thresholds. ## **5.1. Clinical Deployment and Translational Implications** 

The proposed framework has been designed with clinical deployment and real-world applicability in mind. The CNN/LSTM models exhibit low computational complexity and require only marginal training and inference time, making them suitable for execution on general-purpose computers as well as resource-constrained platforms such as embedded systems and smartphones. In practical clinical or community-based screening scenarios, the system can be implemented using lightweight wearable inertial measurement unit (IMU) sensors, which are already widely adopted in gait and mobility assessments. Reliable inference can be achieved with a minimal recording protocol of approximately 5 seconds of continuous walking data. Given that a typical gait cycle lasts about one second and consists of multiple sub-phases, this recording duration captures several consecutive gait cycles, providing sufficient temporal context for stable gait characterization. At an IMU sampling frequency of 100 Hz, a 5-second window yields adequate signal resolution to represent gait dynamics accurately and to distinguish between fallers and non-fallers. Furthermore, inference is performed in near real time, enabling on-device processing without the need for cloud-based computation. These characteristics support a clear translational pathway from controlled experimental evaluation to clinical screening, home-based monitoring, and integration into portable or wearable health technologies, thereby strengthening the clinical relevance of the proposed approach. 

## **6. Conclusion** 

This study demonstrates that deep learning models, particularly CNNs and LSTMs, substantially outperform traditional machine learning approaches for IMU-based fall history classification, achieving 95-96% accuracy under data-level crossvalidation and 87-92% under stringent subject-level validation. Strong performance on secondary tasks (age-group classification and subject identification) validates that these architectures learn robust, generalizable gait representations applicable to diverse prediction problems beyond binary classification. Crucially, LIME analysis of the CNN model for fall history classification, the primary clinical focus, revealed that classification decisions relied on biomechanically plausible gait phase signatures: stance phase irregularities (particularly terminal stance and mid-stance) dominated faller classifications (83.8% contribution), reflecting impaired weight transfer, balance instability, and inefficient propulsion, whereas non-fallers exhibited more balanced stance-swing contributions (61.7% vs. 38.3%), reflecting coordinated gait dynamics and adequate foot clearance.
